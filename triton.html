About Triton

Triton, the largest of Neptune’s moons, was discovered by British astronomer William Lassell in 1846, just 17 days after Neptune was identified. It is unique among large moons in the solar system for its retrograde orbit, meaning it moves in the opposite direction of Neptune’s rotation. This suggests that Triton is a captured object, possibly originating from the Kuiper Belt.

With a diameter of 2,706 kilometers (1,680 miles), Triton is the seventh-largest moon in the solar system. Its surface is primarily composed of nitrogen ice, with active geysers that spew nitrogen gas, making it one of the few geologically active moons.

Triton’s terrain features a mix of smooth volcanic plains and rugged regions, including large fissures and impact craters. The moon’s thin atmosphere, mainly nitrogen with traces of methane, adds to its fascinating character.

Orbiting Neptune at a distance of about 354,800 km (220,500 miles), Triton’s frigid surface temperature of -235°C (-391°F) makes it one of the coldest objects in the solar system.
