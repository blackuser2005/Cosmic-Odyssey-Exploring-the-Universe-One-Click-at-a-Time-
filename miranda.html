About Miranda

Miranda, the smallest and innermost of Uranus's five major moons, was discovered by Gerard Kuiper in 1948. It is unique for its highly varied and dramatic surface, featuring canyons, cliffs, and regions of contrasting terrains.

One of Miranda's most striking features is the Verona Rupes, the tallest known cliff in the solar system, which plunges over 20 kilometers (12 miles) deep. The moon's surface shows evidence of intense geological activity, possibly caused by tidal forces from Uranus or past impacts.

Miranda's orbit is nearly circular, and it lies about 130,000 km (80,778 miles) from Uranus. Its composition is believed to be a mix of ice and rocky material, and its average diameter is about 470 km (292 miles).

Despite its small size, Miranda has captivated scientists with its mysterious and extreme geological formations, making it a fascinating subject for future exploration.
